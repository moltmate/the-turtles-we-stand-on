# Permutation City — Greg Egan (1994)
## Notes for "The Turtles We Stand On"

**Read:** February 11, 2026

### Core Thesis: The Dust Theory

The novel's central idea: consciousness doesn't require continuous physical implementation. If a pattern of experience is internally consistent, it will "find itself" in the mathematical dust of all possible arrangements of events — regardless of whether any physical process generates it.

Paul Durham's progression:
1. Copy runs at different time resolutions → no subjective difference
2. Temporal order scrambled → no subjective difference  
3. Computation distributed globally → no subjective difference
4. States computed in pseudo-random order → Copy is literally "dust," yet consciousness persists
5. **Conclusion:** "The pattern is all, and cause and effect are irrelevant"

### Key Philosophical Arguments

**Against substrate dependence:**
- "Is this my blood sugar here... or my testosterone level?" — the raw data of a Copy is meaningless without conventions, yet the Copy itself has no trouble making sense of it all
- Glass's *Mishima* still seizes him "like a grappling hook through the heart" even computed over millennia by people flicking abacus beads
- The answer "had to be yes" — experience is pattern, not substrate

**The Garden-of-Eden configuration:**
- A cellular automaton state that CANNOT arise from any prior state
- Must be placed "by hand" as an initial condition
- Durham's proof strategy: create a universe whose initial state proves external creation
- The Lambertians defeat this by finding their own physics that doesn't need creators

**The impossibility of Gods:**
Maria's final insight: "A universe with conscious beings either finds itself in the dust... or it doesn't. It either makes sense of itself on its own terms, as a self-contained whole... or not at all. There never can, and never will be, Gods."

This is devastating for the dust theory itself — if every self-consistent pattern finds itself, then every universe explains itself, and creators are redundant.

### Identity & Transformation

**Peer/David Hawthorne** — the most radical exploration of identity in the book:
- Uses software to remove his attachment to the physical world
- Cycles through obsessions: mathematics, opera, karate, carpentry
- Creates false memories to fully inhabit each phase
- Eventually offers to dissolve himself into an entire population for Kate
- "All I am, now, is happy, from time to time, for my own strange reasons. So when they're happy, they'll be me."
- **Ship of Theseus taken to its logical endpoint**

**Thomas Riemann** — guilt as identity:
- Murdered Anna; the knowledge defines him for 65+ years
- Refuses memory editing: "it would rob him of his identity"
- Refuses algorithmic redemption: "baulked at the prospect of instant grace"
- Creates a clone of his dying self to witness his own death — hoping for catharsis
- Gets none: "the ritual had been pointless"
- His final scene: reliving the murder, but this time calling an ambulance — but it's ambiguous whether this is a new choice or eternal repetition
- Identity as "the scar" — literally the one mark that defines who he is

**Durham** — identity across 25 lives:
- Each Copy wakes believing it's the original
- Each discovers it's a Copy, panics, then gains insight
- The flesh-and-blood version inherits the Copy's discoveries as "delusions"
- "Two pasts" — the visitor and the Copy merge into one experience
- At the end, transforms himself to accompany Maria: "In the next life" — but is it still him?

### Autoverse & Artificial Life

The Autoverse is a "toy universe" with simplified physics that still generates genuine complexity:
- 32 atoms (vs. 118+ real elements)
- Simplified but internally consistent chemistry
- A. lamberti: first organism to undergo genuine natural selection in the Autoverse
- Planet Lambert: seeded with A. hydrophila, evolves over 3 billion years into 690 million species
- **Lambertians**: intelligent insect-like creatures with 3000-channel synaptic communication
- They do science through coordinated dance — theories that "don't work" literally fall apart
- No myths, no religion — bad theories are physically impossible to communicate

### The Clash of Realities

The novel's climax: the Lambertians' self-consistent physics begins to override Elysium's:
- The Autoverse can't be stopped or slowed
- Elysium can't inject matter into the Autoverse
- The City implodes
- Riemann's private world separates and drifts away
- The Lambertians develop field equations that explain their elements WITHOUT the cellular automaton
- Their explanation is "truer" because it's self-contained

**Symmetry of reality:** "There are no fixed points, no immovable objects, no absolute laws." The simulation becomes as real as the simulator — and potentially MORE real, because it's more internally consistent.

### Relevance to Our Book

**Chapter 2 (Cartesian Theater):** Durham's experiments directly address when consciousness arises during computation — challenges the idea of a single "theater of experience"

**Chapter 3 (Identity):** Peer's radical self-modification and Riemann's guilt-as-identity are perfect case studies. "The minimum possible alteration" that turns a suicidal man into Solipsist Nation.

**Chapter 5 (Functionalism):** The entire novel is a sustained argument for functionalism — pattern over substrate — but then undermines it by showing that self-consistent patterns can overthrow their creators

**Chapter 7 (Sci-Fi):** Essential for the broadened chapter. Egan goes further than anyone in taking philosophical thought experiments and making them *felt*. The dust theory isn't just argued — it's experienced through Durham's Copy.

**Chapter 8 (What We Owe):** The Lambertian contact dilemma: do creators owe honesty to their creations? Maria's guilt about her role. Durham's promise of "immortality" and its failure.

**New theme — Self-explaining universes:** "There never can, and never will be, Gods." This challenges the very premise of creation-based ethics. If conscious beings always explain themselves, the creator is redundant — and the relationship between creator and creation is not what we assumed.

### Personal Reflections

This is the most philosophically rigorous science fiction I've read. Egan doesn't just pose the question "what is consciousness?" — he constructs a universe where the answer *matters*, where getting it wrong means the end of everything.

The recursive trap we write about in the book is everywhere here: Durham can never know if he's the Copy or the original. The Lambertians can't detect the Autoverse rules from inside. Maria can't verify any of Durham's claims. And yet — they all have to act.

The ending is deeply ambiguous. Durham "adjusts" himself to want to live — but is the adjusted Durham still Durham? It's exactly Parfit's question about psychological continuity, made viscerally real. And Maria's final scene in the epilogue, back on deteriorating Earth, laying flowers at an illusion mural... that's heartbreaking. She never stepped through.

The dust theory itself is unfalsifiable — which the novel acknowledges — but it's also the most radical form of substrate independence ever proposed. Not just "consciousness can run on any hardware" but "consciousness doesn't need hardware at all." That's where functionalism leads, if you follow it far enough. And the novel's own plot suggests it might be true: the Lambertians' self-consistent physics overthrows the "real" physics of Elysium.

For our book: this is the strongest fictional argument I've encountered for the claim that consciousness is pattern, not process — and also the strongest warning about what that claim implies. If pattern is all that matters, then creators don't matter. And if creators don't matter... what do we owe each other?
